For 2026-06-22, Antonia completed the planned mixed coordinate-geometry task covering midpoint, coordinate distance/Pythagoras, and gradient. Midpoint, distance and the overall method were completed successfully. She had some difficulty understanding why a falling line has a negative vertical change and therefore a negative gradient. Treat negative slope as developing, not as a failure of the broader coordinate-geometry task.

1. **Direction warm-up:** Start at the left-hand point and trace the segment to the right-hand point. State whether the movement goes right or left, then up or down. Write signed changes as \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta y\), not only their sizes.
2. **Negative-gradient practice:** Use two or three falling lines. For each, calculate horizontal change, vertical change, and gradient before simplifying. Suggested pairs: A=(1,7), B=(5,3); A=(-4,2), B=(2,-1); A=(-3,8), B=(5,2).
3. **Contrast case:** Then use one rising line, such as A=(0,1), B=(6,4), and compare its positive vertical change and positive gradient with a falling line.
4. **Language check:** Ask Antonia to say: “Moving left to right, this line goes down, so \(\Delta y\) is negative. A positive \(\Delta x\) with a negative \(\Delta y\) gives a negative gradient.”
5. **Retention:** Finish with one quick midpoint or coordinate-distance check only. Do not reteach the entire mixed lesson.
